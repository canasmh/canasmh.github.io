About Me

"Somewhere, something incredible is waiting to be known." - Carl Sagan

I am a second year graduate student of Astronomy at New Mexico State University. I was born in Colombia, but moved to the United States at a very young age. I grew up in Greenville, South Carolina, which I will always consider to be my hometown. After graduating high school, I moved to Charleston, SC to attend the College of Charleston. I was initially interested in business and economics, but quickly discovered that my passion lies in Physics and Astronomy. As such, I joined the department of Physics and Astronomy, working under the mentorship of Dr. George Chartas, where I studied quasar outflows through the use of X-ray data obtained with the XMM-Newton and Chandra X-ray space observatories. In 2018, I graduated from the College of Charleston with a B.S. in Astrophysics and a B.S. in Physics.

After graduating from the College of Charleston, I spent a few months in Jackson Hole, Wyoming working for a 501(c)3 non-profit organization known as Wyoming Stargazing. I worked as a Stargazing Leader, and it was my job to proivde an unforgettable tour of the night sky to tourists and locals through the use of telescopes and powerful laser pointers. The job was an excellent way for me to become acquainted with some of the most popular constellations, the brightest and most significant stars, and some of the most prominent deep sky objects. As such, I become very familiar with the location of objects in the night sky, and gained a tremendous amount of practice in explaining complex astrophysical phenomena to a general public.

In the fall of 2019, I chose to go New Mexico State University because of the profound impacts the Apache Point Observatory and Sloan Digital Sky Survey have had in the astronomical community. I also greatly appreciated that the founder of the Astronomy department at NMSU was none other than Clyde Tombaugh, the discoverer of the dwarf planet, Pluto. Since my admission, I have been working with Dr. Lyra studying the origin of Kuiper belt objects through the streaming instability of ices and silicates.
